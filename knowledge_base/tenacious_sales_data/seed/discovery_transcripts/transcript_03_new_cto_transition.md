# Discovery Call Transcript 03 — New CTO Transition (Segment 3)

**Synthetic transcript.** Composed from the Tenacious sales team's style. Not a real call.

**Prospect:** Priya Narayanan, newly appointed CTO (65 days in role), synthetic Series C B2B SaaS company (~180 employees).
**Tenacious lead:** Arun Sharma, Co-founder.
**Length:** ~22 minutes.
**Outcome:** Agreed to a second conversation with Priya's head of infrastructure present.

---

**Arun:** Priya, thanks for making the time. Congratulations on the CTO role. I saw the announcement in March.

**Priya:** Thanks. Two months in. Still learning where the bodies are buried.

**Arun:** That's actually the reason I reached out. In our experience with CTO transitions, the first 90 days are when vendor mix gets a fresh look. I don't want to sell you anything today — I want to understand what your first-90-days reassessment looks like, and whether managed offshore delivery is a piece you're actively thinking about.

**Priya:** It's on the list but not at the top. What's at the top is the observability stack — we have four monitoring tools and nobody knows which alerts are real. Vendor consolidation for observability is where I'm putting the first decision.

**Arun:** Fair. Let me ask — when you did think about offshore or distributed delivery coming into the role, what was your prior view?

**Priya:** Skeptical, honestly. My previous company used Accenture for a big migration and it was a slog. High communication overhead, ramp time was forever, nobody felt accountable. The CTO before me here used contractors on Upwork, which I don't love either.

**Arun:** Both of those are reasonable things to be skeptical about. Can I ask a more specific question — if you could structure offshore delivery to avoid the specific failures you saw at the previous company, what would have needed to be different?

**Priya:** The Accenture team rotated constantly. We never had the same engineers for more than three months. That meant every time we had a hard design decision, we explained the context again. Also the project manager layer added a translation loss — we couldn't talk directly to the engineers doing the work.

**Arun:** Both of those are solvable. Our engineers are employees, not contractors — we have an 18-month average tenure and we explicitly commit to stability of the named engineers on your account. And our project managers are coordinators, not gatekeepers; the named engineers are in your Slack, your standups, your code review.

**Priya:** That's better. What's the catch?

**Arun:** Time zone. Our team is in Ethiopia, which gives us three to five hours of overlap with your East Coast team. For US West Coast it's a stretch — doable but requires deliberate cadence design.

**Priya:** Most of my team is in New York and Boston. Offshore Europe has worked for us before.

**Arun:** Good. Second catch — we are most useful for clients who have a clear architectural direction internally and need delivery capacity on top. We're not the team to outsource architecture to. If you're still forming your architecture thesis for a specific product area, that's in-house work.

**Priya:** That's good to hear actually. I wasn't looking to outsource thinking.

**Arun:** Given your priority is observability consolidation, we probably aren't the next conversation for you. But if, say, your consolidation work opens up capacity questions in data platform or backend services, we'd want to be part of that conversation.

**Priya:** Actually — and this is useful context — the observability project will consume my infrastructure director for the next quarter. Backend work that was supposed to land in Q3 doesn't have clear ownership right now.

**Arun:** That's where we can be useful. I'd suggest a second conversation with your head of infrastructure present, so we can talk specifically about what backend work might make sense for a managed squad while your director is focused on observability. Would that be workable in the next two weeks?

**Priya:** My head of infrastructure is Anita Cole. Let me check her calendar. Probably yes, late next week.

**Arun:** Happy to set it up. One thing I want to flag — we should keep the second conversation low-pressure. No proposal yet, just a scoping conversation with Anita to understand whether a specific backend workload maps to our bench. If after that call it makes sense, we move to written proposal. If not, no hard feelings.

**Priya:** That cadence works.

**Arun:** Good. I'll send a Cal link for three times next week with you and Anita included.

**Priya:** One more thing. I looked at your public materials and you mention "100% African talent." Is that a deliberate positioning or just a current state?

**Arun:** Deliberate. We're a public-benefit company registered in Delaware. Our core mission is building a pipeline of high-agency engineering talent out of African universities. It has practical implications for our clients — engineers tend to be more loyal to the company because the career path is differentiated — and it has social implications for the team. It's not a diversity-washing line; it's the primary recruiting filter.

**Priya:** Appreciated. I wanted to understand what the line meant before I passed materials to Anita.

**Arun:** Thanks for asking directly. Talk next week.

## What the call demonstrates

- **Segment 3 opens with the transition as the anchor.** The AI-maturity score does not shift this pitch — Arun leads with the new-CTO context and asks about the reassessment shape.
- **Explicit invitation to name prior bad experiences.** "What would have needed to be different" is a high-leverage question for Segment 3. New CTOs are reassessing precisely because of past bad patterns; naming them makes the conversation real.
- **Willing to defer if the fit isn't the top priority.** Arun tells Priya offshore may not be the next conversation, which signals trust-worthy rather than sales-y.
- **Specific next step with the right additional person.** The head of infrastructure (Anita) is the correct second-call attendee for the scoping conversation. Arun names the escalation explicitly.
- **Direct answer to the 100% African talent question.** Prospects sometimes probe positioning; the answer must be specific and grounded rather than defensive.
