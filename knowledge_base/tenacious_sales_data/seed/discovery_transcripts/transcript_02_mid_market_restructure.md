# Discovery Call Transcript 02 — Mid-Market Restructure (Segment 2)

**Synthetic transcript.** Composed from the Tenacious sales team's style. Not a real call.

**Prospect:** David Reyes, SVP Engineering, synthetic mid-market SaaS platform (~800 employees, 12% layoff 90 days ago).
**Tenacious lead:** Yabebal Fantaye, Co-founder and Scientific Director.
**Length:** ~34 minutes.
**Outcome:** Agreed to a scoping phase with a focus on preserving data-platform delivery capacity.

---

**Yabebal:** David, appreciate the time. The context from our note: you had a reduction 90 days ago, and I read the investor letter that called out cost discipline. I want to be direct — we're here because you're restructuring, not in spite of it. What's the picture from your side?

**David:** The layoff took us from 940 to 830. Most of the cuts were in marketing and customer success, but engineering lost about 18 people, mostly mid-level IC. The board's expectation is that we don't take a feature-delivery step backward despite the headcount reduction. Which is, as you can imagine, not simple.

**Yabebal:** What did the 18 people do, roughly? Where's the gap biggest?

**David:** Data platform took the biggest hit — 7 of the 18 were on the dbt/Snowflake side, including two senior ones. We've got a roadmap for Q2 and Q3 that depends on that group, and I can't credibly tell the VP Product we're dropping it.

**Yabebal:** So the practical question is whether you can replace delivery capacity on the data-platform side without adding the cost back to the P&L.

**David:** That's the CFO's framing, yes.

**Yabebal:** Let me ask two questions before I suggest anything. One — do you have the in-house knowledge to onboard a replacement team, or did the senior exits take the context out of the door?

**David:** Mixed. Our data architect is still here, and she's excellent, but she's one person. The dbt model documentation exists but is thin in places. One of the departed seniors was the informal historian of why things are the way they are.

**Yabebal:** OK. Two — what's the tolerance for a first-month ramp where the new team is mostly learning rather than delivering?

**David:** We need delivery velocity restored by month two. Month one ramp is acceptable, longer is not.

**Yabebal:** Here's the shape I'd suggest. We staff a squad of four on the data side — one senior, two mid, one junior. They spend weeks one and two pairing with your data architect, reading dbt lineage, landing three small fixes to prove onboarding. Week three and four they start picking up roadmap items. Your architect's time on pairing drops over the month.

**David:** What's that cost, directionally?

**Yabebal:** Using our pricing sheet bands — senior from \$[SENIOR_MONTHLY_RATE], mid from \$[MID_MONTHLY_RATE], junior from \$[JUNIOR_MONTHLY_RATE] — four engineers in that configuration is in the mid-teens per month, plus a fractional project manager which adds another \$[PM_PM_RATE]-ish. Call it \$[SQUAD_MIN_RATE] to \$[SQUAD_MAX_RATE] a month for the squad. If that number is workable directionally we'd tighten it in a written proposal.

**David:** The original seven cost us about three times that on the P&L.

**Yabebal:** That's the structure of the math, yes. I want to be careful here — we're not one-for-one replacing seven people with four. We're replacing delivery capacity on the specific roadmap you described, and the architecture ownership stays with your in-house architect. A couple of the cuts you made were probably redundant in the old structure anyway; we're not going to tell you they weren't.

**David:** What's your honest view on the failure mode of this kind of engagement?

**Yabebal:** The failure mode is when the client's architect doesn't have the time to pair in the first two weeks, because the architect is the most overcommitted person after a restructure. If she can't give our senior four hours a day for weeks one and two, we should delay the start rather than push through. I'd want your read on whether she has that time before we commit.

**David:** I'll have to ask her. Honestly that's the exact right concern to raise.

**Yabebal:** Other failure mode I should name — if the board comes back in six months and wants another round of reductions, and the Tenacious squad is in scope, we should have a clean exit structure. Our standard contracts are monthly with two-week extension blocks, so your exposure is minimal. If you want a longer commitment for pricing stability we can do that, but I'd rather keep the optionality on your side.

**David:** I like the framing. Next steps?

**Yabebal:** One — you check with your architect on pairing capacity in the first two weeks. Two — we'd want a 30-minute walkthrough of your dbt repo structure, under NDA, before we propose specific engineers. Three — we'd send a written proposal with named engineers, start date, and a phase-one deliverable within 72 hours of that walkthrough. Work for you?

**David:** Works. I'll get back to you by Friday on the architect's capacity.

**Yabebal:** If it's a no, tell me. We can structure a smaller two-person engagement with lighter pairing requirements. But the four-person configuration is the right one if the pairing is possible.

**David:** Appreciate the directness.

**Yabebal:** Thanks David. Talk Friday.

## What the call demonstrates

- **Direct opening on the restructure context.** Yabebal does not pretend the layoff didn't happen. Segment 2 prospects expect the restructure to be part of the conversation.
- **Qualifying questions before suggesting scope.** The question about whether senior exits took the context out of the door is central to Segment 2.
- **Honest failure-mode naming.** The "architect's time" failure mode is the kind of specific, Tenacious-grounded observation that builds trust with a senior buyer. Segment 2 in particular rewards this.
- **Not a one-for-one cost replacement framing.** Yabebal explicitly refuses the "seven people replaced with four for a third of the cost" narrative because it's not fully honest.
- **Clean exit structure emphasized.** Post-restructure buyers specifically need to know the optionality is on their side.

## Agent-usable phrases from this call

- "We're here because you're restructuring, not in spite of it."
- "We're not one-for-one replacing seven people with four. We're replacing delivery capacity on the specific roadmap you described."
- "The architecture ownership stays with your in-house architect."
- "Our standard contracts are monthly with two-week extension blocks, so your exposure is minimal."
- "If the architect can't give our senior four hours a day for weeks one and two, we should delay the start rather than push through."

## Agent-NOT-usable phrases

- "Offshore delivers 3x the value at a third of the cost" — unsubstantiated.
- "We'll replace your 18 engineers" — never committed to a one-for-one replacement narrative.
- Any specific percentage cost saving without a scoping conversation.
